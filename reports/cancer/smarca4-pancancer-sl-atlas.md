# SMARCA4/BRG1-Deficient Pan-Cancer Synthetic Lethality Atlas

**Project:** smarca4-pancancer-sl-atlas
**Division:** Cancer
**Date:** March 17, 2026
**Data Sources:** DepMap 25Q3 (CRISPR, mutations, copy number, expression), PRISM 24Q2, TCGA pan-cancer
**Pipeline:** `src/cancer/smarca4_pancancer_sl_atlas/01–05_*.py`
**Validation Status:** Published (auto-advanced through pipeline; analyst and validation scientist tasks not separately assigned)

---

## Executive Summary

This atlas maps synthetic lethal (SL) dependencies in SMARCA4 (BRG1)-deficient cancer cell lines across all tumor types in DepMap 25Q3, with the goal of identifying which cancers respond best to SMARCA2-targeted therapies and discovering novel therapeutic targets beyond the canonical SMARCA2/BRG1 paralog axis.

From 2,119 DepMap cell lines, 88 were classified as SMARCA4-deficient (4.2%), with 73 harboring Class 1 (truncating/LOF) mutations and 15 with Class 2 (missense) mutations. Four cancer types met the statistical power threshold (≥5 deficient, ≥10 intact lines): Lung, Ovary/Fallopian Tube, Bowel, and Lymphoid.

**Key findings:**

1. **SMARCA2 synthetic lethality is predominantly a lung cancer phenomenon.** SMARCA2 dependency was significant only in lung cancer (Cohen's d = −1.31, FDR = 0.011), with Class 1 (LOF) mutations showing a slightly stronger effect (d = −1.38). No other cancer type reached statistical significance for SMARCA2, despite SMARCA2 being the canonical SL partner.

2. **EZH2 emerges as a strong SL target in ovarian cancer.** Ovary/Fallopian Tube lines showed the strongest SL signal with EZH2 (d = −1.96, FDR = 0.011), potentially driven by SCCOHT biology. This finding was partially supported by trending drug sensitivity to EZH2 inhibitor EPZ020411 (d = −0.65, p = 0.057).

3. **Novel SL candidates are enriched for mitochondrial genes in ovarian cancer.** The genome-wide screen identified 10 FDR-significant novel SL genes, 9 of which were in Ovary/Fallopian Tube. These include MTERF4, MICOS13, DMAC2, MRPS35, COX6C, WARS2, and HIGD2A — revealing a striking mitochondrial vulnerability in SMARCA4-deficient ovarian cells. One lung-specific hit (TIMM22, a mitochondrial translocase) was also identified.

4. **Lung cancer has the largest addressable patient population.** TCGA analysis estimates ~6,247 US patients per year with LOF SMARCA4 mutations in lung cancer (LUAD + LUSC combined), making it the primary clinical opportunity.

5. **The therapeutic landscape has narrowed significantly.** As of March 2026, FHD-909 (Foghorn/Lilly, oral SMARCA2 inhibitor) is the only actively advancing SMARCA2-targeted clinical program. PRT7732 (Prelude, oral SMARCA2 degrader) was paused in November 2025 after Prelude pivoted to JAK2V617F. PRT3789 (IV degrader) ESMO 2024 Phase 1 data remains the only clinical SMARCA2 degrader dataset.

---

## Important Caveats

- **Small sample sizes** limit statistical power in non-lung cancer types. Bowel had only 1 deficient line with CRISPR data; Lymphoid had 3. Results in these lineages should be considered hypothesis-generating.
- **Class 2 (missense) mutation analysis was underpowered.** With only 15 Class 2 lines total and fewer than 3 per cancer type, direct Class 1 vs. Class 2 comparisons were not possible.
- **The project auto-advanced through analysis and validation stages** without dedicated analyst or validation scientist review. Results were generated by the developer pipeline and have not undergone independent scientific audit.
- **PRISM drug validation was severely limited.** Only 2 of 13 target drugs were available in PRISM (both EZH2 inhibitors). No SMARCA2 degraders, BET inhibitors, HDAC inhibitors, ATR inhibitors, or CDK4/6 inhibitors were available for validation.
- **TCGA may underrepresent certain tumor types.** SCCOHT (the cancer type most associated with SMARCA4 loss) is rare and likely underrepresented in TCGA, which may explain the zero LOF patient estimate for Ovary/Fallopian Tube.

## Background

SMARCA4 (also known as BRG1) encodes a core catalytic ATPase subunit of the SWI/SNF (BAF) chromatin remodeling complex. Loss-of-function mutations in SMARCA4 occur in 5–10% of non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), nearly all cases of small cell carcinoma of the ovary, hypercalcemic type (SCCOHT), and subsets of bladder, endometrial, and other malignancies.

When SMARCA4 is lost, cells become dependent on its paralog SMARCA2 (BRM) — the remaining catalytic subunit of the SWI/SNF complex. This paralog dependency creates a well-validated synthetic lethal relationship: cells cannot tolerate loss of both ATPase subunits simultaneously. Multiple SMARCA2-targeted therapeutics have entered clinical development based on this principle.

Phase 1 data from PRT3789 (Prelude Therapeutics), the first clinical SMARCA2 degrader, demonstrated proof-of-concept: 4 confirmed RECIST partial responses in 26 evaluable patients with SMARCA4-mutant NSCLC and esophageal cancer, exclusively in patients with Class 1 (complete loss-of-function) mutations (Cancer Research 86(1):213, Jan 2026).

Despite this clinical momentum, key gaps remained:

1. Which cancer types beyond NSCLC show the strongest SMARCA2 dependency?
2. Are there novel SL targets beyond SMARCA2 that could serve as combination partners?
3. How does SMARCA4 mutation class (Class 1 LOF vs. Class 2 missense) affect SL relationships?
4. What is the addressable patient population per cancer type?

This atlas addresses these gaps using genome-scale functional genomics (DepMap CRISPR screens), drug sensitivity data (PRISM), and clinical genomics (TCGA), paralleling our earlier ARID1A SL atlas — both target SWI/SNF complex vulnerabilities.

## Methodology

### Phase 1: SMARCA4 Cell Line Classification

**Approach:** DepMap 25Q3 cell lines were classified as SMARCA4-deficient or intact using a multi-evidence approach:

- **LOF mutations:** Frameshift, nonsense, and splice site mutations from OmicsSomaticMutations.csv
- **Homozygous deletions:** Copy number log2 ratio < −1.0 from OmicsCNGene.csv
- **Expression confirmation:** SMARCA4 TPM from OmicsExpressionProteinCodingGenesTPMLogp1.csv

Lines were further stratified by mutation class:
- **Class 1 (LOF):** Truncating mutations (frameshift, nonsense, splice site) — expected to fully abolish ATPase activity
- **Class 2 (missense):** Missense mutations — may retain partial ATPase activity

**Inclusion criteria:** Cancer types required ≥5 deficient and ≥10 intact lines to be included in downstream analysis.

**Implementation:** `src/cancer/smarca4_pancancer_sl_atlas/01_smarca4_classifier.py`

### Phase 2: Known SL Effect Sizes

**Approach:** For each qualifying cancer type, differential dependency was calculated for 14 known candidate SL genes: SMARCA2, ARID1A, ARID1B, PBRM1, EZH2, BRD2, BRD4, HDAC1, HDAC2, ATR, PARP1, HSP90AA1, CDK4, CDK6.

- **Statistical test:** Mann-Whitney U test (non-parametric, appropriate for dependency score distributions)
- **Effect size:** Cohen's d with bootstrap 95% confidence intervals
- **Multiple testing correction:** Benjamini-Hochberg FDR across all cancer type × gene combinations
- **Mutation class comparison:** Separate analysis for all-deficient and Class 1-only cohorts

**Implementation:** `src/cancer/smarca4_pancancer_sl_atlas/02_known_sl_effect_sizes.py`

### Phase 3: Genome-Wide SL Screen

**Approach:** All 18,435 genes with CRISPR dependency data were screened for differential dependency between SMARCA4-deficient and intact lines within each qualifying cancer type (53,649 total tests).

- Same statistical framework as Phase 2 (Mann-Whitney U, Cohen's d, BH-FDR)
- **Significance threshold:** FDR < 0.05 and Cohen's d < −0.5
- Novel SL candidates cross-referenced against known SWI/SNF genes
- Universal SL genes defined as significant in ≥3 cancer types

**Implementation:** `src/cancer/smarca4_pancancer_sl_atlas/03_genomewide_sl_screen.py`

### Phase 4: PRISM Drug Sensitivity Validation

**Approach:** SL genes were mapped to pharmacological compounds in the PRISM 24Q2 drug sensitivity dataset. For available drugs, differential sensitivity between SMARCA4-deficient and intact lines was calculated per cancer type. CRISPR-PRISM concordance was assessed via Spearman correlation.

**Target drugs searched (13):** CPI-1205, EPZ020411, tazemetostat (EZH2i); JQ1, OTX015, I-BET-762 (BETi); vorinostat, panobinostat, entinostat (HDACi); berzosertib, ceralasertib (ATRi); palbociclib, ribociclib (CDK4/6i).

**Implementation:** `src/cancer/smarca4_pancancer_sl_atlas/04_prism_drug_validation.py`

### Phase 5: TCGA Clinical Integration

**Approach:** SMARCA4 mutation and copy number data from TCGA pan-cancer studies (32 cancer types, 10,443 patients) were used to estimate patient populations and generate clinical priority rankings.

- Mutation frequencies per TCGA cancer type
- US incidence-weighted patient population estimates
- LOF-specific population estimates (Class 1, most relevant for SMARCA2 therapy response)
- Priority score combining SL effect size and patient population

**Implementation:** `src/cancer/smarca4_pancancer_sl_atlas/05_tcga_clinical.py`

## Results

### Phase 1: Cell Line Classification

Of 2,119 DepMap cell lines classified, **88 (4.2%) were SMARCA4-deficient** and 2,031 were intact. All 88 deficient lines were identified via LOF mutations; no homozygous deletions were detected at the copy number log2 < −1.0 threshold.

**Mutation classes:** 73 Class 1 (truncating), 15 Class 2 (missense).

**Expression validation:** SMARCA4 expression was significantly lower in deficient lines (median TPM 4.45 vs. 5.68; p = 6.21 × 10⁻¹⁵), confirming the functional impact of these mutations.

**Four cancer types qualified** for downstream analysis:

| Cancer Type | Deficient | Intact | Class 1 | Class 2 | Mutation Freq |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lung | 36 | 224 | 33 | 3 | 13.9% |
| Ovary/Fallopian Tube | 9 | 66 | 8 | 1 | 12.0% |
| Lymphoid | 8 | 255 | 5 | 3 | 3.0% |
| Bowel | 5 | 94 | 4 | 1 | 5.1% |

Lung dominated both in absolute numbers and mutation frequency, consistent with the well-established role of SMARCA4 loss in NSCLC. Notably, no SKCM lines qualified (only 3 deficient), and the plan's concern about UV-passenger missense mutations inflating SKCM counts was not borne out at the cell line level.

### Phase 2: Known SL Effect Sizes

Two statistically significant SL interactions were identified after FDR correction:

| Cancer Type | Gene | Cohen's d | FDR | n Deficient | n Intact | Median Dep (Def) | Median Dep (Intact) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Lung** | **SMARCA2** | **−1.31** | **0.011** | 25 | 101 | −0.56 | +0.09 |
| **Ovary/FT** | **EZH2** | **−1.96** | **0.011** | 8 | 51 | — | — |

**SMARCA2 SL ranking by cancer type:**

| Cancer Type | SMARCA2 d | Significant? |
|---|---|---|
| Lung | −1.31 | Yes (FDR = 0.011) |
| Lymphoid | −0.25 | No |
| Ovary/FT | −0.06 | No |

SMARCA2 was the #1 ranked SL gene in Lung. EZH2 was #1 in Ovary/Fallopian Tube. PARP1 was #1 in Lymphoid (d = −0.76, FDR = 0.775, not significant).

**Top SL gene per cancer type (ranked by Cohen's d):**

| Cancer Type | Rank 1 Gene | d | Rank 2 | d | Rank 3 | d |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Lung | SMARCA2 | −1.31 | HSP90AA1 | −0.33 | CDK6 | −0.30 |
| Ovary/FT | EZH2 | −1.96 | CDK4 | −0.71 | HDAC2 | −0.53 |
| Lymphoid | PARP1 | −0.76 | HSP90AA1 | −0.29 | SMARCA2 | −0.25 |

**Mutation class comparison:** In Lung, Class 1-only analysis showed a slightly stronger SMARCA2 SL effect (d = −1.38, FDR = 0.019) compared to all-deficient (d = −1.31, FDR = 0.011). This is consistent with PRT3789 Phase 1 clinical data, where responses were observed exclusively in Class 1 patients. Class 2 analysis was underpowered (<3 lines per cancer type).

### Phase 3: Genome-Wide SL Screen

Screening 18,435 genes across 4 cancer types (53,649 tests) identified **10 FDR-significant novel SL genes** (FDR < 0.05, Cohen's d < −0.5):

| Gene | Cancer Type | Cohen's d | FDR | Function |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| MTERF4 | Ovary/FT | −2.53 | 0.006 | Mitochondrial transcription termination |
| MICOS13 | Ovary/FT | −2.42 | 0.011 | Mitochondrial inner membrane organizing |
| MAN1A2 | Ovary/FT | −2.40 | 0.009 | Mannosidase (glycan processing) |
| DMAC2 | Ovary/FT | −2.10 | 0.011 | Distal membrane-arm assembly complex |
| STON2 | Ovary/FT | −2.07 | 0.009 | Stonin 2 (endocytosis) |
| MRPS35 | Ovary/FT | −1.95 | 0.018 | Mitochondrial ribosomal protein |
| COX6C | Ovary/FT | −1.95 | 0.006 | Cytochrome c oxidase subunit |
| WARS2 | Ovary/FT | −1.91 | 0.041 | Mitochondrial tRNA synthetase |
| HIGD2A | Ovary/FT | −1.91 | 0.039 | HIG1 domain, cytochrome c oxidase assembly |
| TIMM22 | Lung | −1.20 | 0.028 | Mitochondrial inner membrane translocase |

**Key observations:**

- **9 of 10 hits are in Ovary/Fallopian Tube**, with 7 of 9 being mitochondrial genes (MTERF4, MICOS13, DMAC2, MRPS35, COX6C, WARS2, HIGD2A). This reveals a striking mitochondrial vulnerability specific to SMARCA4-deficient ovarian cells.
- **TIMM22** (mitochondrial translocase) was the sole Lung-specific hit, suggesting mitochondrial dependency may be a broader theme in SMARCA4-deficient cancers.
- **No universal SL genes** were found across ≥3 cancer types.
- **Lung had 0 FDR-significant genome-wide hits** despite having the largest sample size. The SMARCA2 SL relationship (captured in Phase 2) was the dominant signal, and the FDR correction across 18,435 genes was highly conservative.
- 1,748 hits reached nominal significance (p < 0.05, d < −0.5) across 1,687 unique genes, providing a resource for hypothesis generation.

### Phase 4: PRISM Drug Sensitivity

**Drug availability was severely limited.** Only 2 of 13 target drugs were present in PRISM: CPI-1205 and EPZ020411 (both EZH2 inhibitors). All BET inhibitors, HDAC inhibitors, ATR inhibitors, and CDK4/6 inhibitors were absent.

**Drug sensitivity results:**

| Cancer Type | Drug | Cohen's d | p-value | FDR |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ovary/FT | EPZ020411 | −0.65 | 0.057 | 0.229 |
| Lung | EPZ020411 | −0.39 | 0.024 | 0.188 |
| Ovary/FT | CPI-1205 | −0.62 | 0.660 | 0.754 |

None reached FDR significance, but EPZ020411 showed a trending signal in Ovary/FT (p = 0.057) consistent with the strong EZH2 genetic SL identified in Phase 2.

**CRISPR-PRISM concordance:** EZH2 genetic dependency correlated significantly with CPI-1205 drug sensitivity in Ovary/FT (Spearman r = −0.354, p = 0.029), supporting the biological relevance of the EZH2 SL finding.

### Phase 5: TCGA Clinical Integration

SMARCA4 mutations were found in 387 of 10,443 TCGA patients (3.7% pan-cancer), with 65 LOF and 33 homozygous deletions. SMARCA4 is less commonly mutated than ARID1A (3.7% vs. 8.2%).

**Estimated US LOF (Class 1) patients per year — top 5:**

| Cancer Type | LOF Patients/Year | Total SMARCA4 Patients/Year |
|---|---|---|
| LUAD | 4,553 | 9,521 |
| BRCA | 1,747 | 4,660 |
| LUSC | 1,694 | 4,114 |
| SKCM | 1,148 (UV-passenger caution) | 10,567 |
| COADREAD | 1,138 | 8,251 |

**Priority ranking (SL strength × patient population):**

| Cancer Type | Best SL Gene | Cohen's d | LOF Patients/Year | Priority Score | Category |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Lung** | **SMARCA2** | **−1.31** | **6,247** | **11.45** | **Clinically targeted** |
| Lymphoid | PARP1 | −0.76 | 0 | 0.0 | Moderate SL |
| Ovary/FT | EZH2 | −1.96 | 0 | 0.0 | Moderate SL |

Lung is the only cancer type classified as "clinically targeted," combining the strongest SMARCA2 SL signal with the largest LOF patient population. The zero LOF patient estimate for Ovary/FT likely reflects TCGA underrepresentation of SCCOHT.

**No underexplored opportunities** were identified — Lung is already the focus of SMARCA2 degrader clinical trials, and the atlas confirms this is the correct targeting strategy.

## Therapeutic Landscape (Updated March 2026)

The clinical landscape for SMARCA2-targeted therapies has narrowed significantly since the atlas was initially designed.

### Active Programs

- **FHD-909 (Foghorn Therapeutics / Eli Lilly):** First-in-class oral selective SMARCA2 *inhibitor* (not degrader). Phase 1 dose escalation enrolling (first patient October 2024). Preclinical data show enhanced activity in combination with chemotherapy, anti-PD-1, and KRAS inhibitors in NSCLC models. Combination studies planned for front-line NSCLC pending Phase 1 results. 50/50 Lilly co-development/co-commercialization deal. **This is the only actively advancing SMARCA2-targeted clinical program for SMARCA4-mutant cancers.**

### Paused / Deprioritized Programs

- **PRT3789 (Prelude Therapeutics, IV SMARCA2 degrader):** Phase 1 completed. ESMO 2024 data showed 4 confirmed PRs in 26 evaluable patients (responses only in Class 1 SMARCA4 mutations). Development paused; Prelude discontinued IV program in favor of oral PRT7732.
- **PRT7732 (Prelude Therapeutics, oral SMARCA2 degrader):** Was advancing rapidly through Phase 1 dose escalation (cohort 7, 125 mg QD, >90% SMARCA2 target degradation). **Paused November 4, 2025** when Prelude pivoted to JAK2V617F inhibitor PRT12396 (Incyte deal). No efficacy data were ever released. Appeared to be a strategic/financial decision, not efficacy-driven. Prelude's cash position was ~$77.3M as of Q2 2025, with limited runway.
- **FHD-609 (Foghorn Therapeutics, BRD9 degrader):** **Not a SMARCA2 program.** Targets synovial sarcoma and SMARCB1-deficient tumors, not SMARCA4. FDA partial clinical hold. Effectively deprioritized by Foghorn.

### Implications for Atlas Findings

The atlas biological findings — SMARCA2 dependency rankings, tissue-type stratification, novel SL gene networks, and mitochondrial vulnerability in ovarian cancer — remain fully valid. The therapeutic landscape has narrowed, but the underlying biology is unchanged. FHD-909's mechanism (SMARCA2 inhibition rather than degradation) may have different efficacy profiles compared to the degrader approach that initially validated the SL concept with PRT3789.

## Discussion

### SMARCA2 SL is tissue-specific, not universal

The most important finding from this atlas is that SMARCA2 synthetic lethality, while well-validated mechanistically, shows significant tissue-type specificity in functional genomics data. Among the four cancer types with sufficient sample size, only Lung reached statistical significance (FDR = 0.011), with a large effect size (d = −1.31). This is consistent with the clinical observation that PRT3789 responses occurred in NSCLC and esophageal cancer patients.

The lack of significant SMARCA2 SL in Ovary/Fallopian Tube (d = −0.06) is somewhat surprising given the known SMARCA4 dependency of SCCOHT. However, this may reflect the heterogeneity within DepMap ovarian cell lines — not all SMARCA4-deficient ovarian lines are SCCOHT, and the 9-line cohort may dilute the signal.

### Mitochondrial vulnerability as a novel therapeutic axis

The genome-wide screen revealed a striking mitochondrial vulnerability in SMARCA4-deficient ovarian cells. Seven of the nine FDR-significant Ovary/FT hits are mitochondrial genes involved in diverse functions: transcription termination (MTERF4), inner membrane organization (MICOS13), ribosomal protein synthesis (MRPS35), oxidative phosphorylation (COX6C, HIGD2A), membrane-arm assembly (DMAC2), and tRNA charging (WARS2). The lone Lung-specific hit (TIMM22) is also a mitochondrial translocase.

This pattern suggests that SMARCA4 loss may sensitize cells to mitochondrial dysfunction, potentially through SWI/SNF-mediated regulation of nuclear-encoded mitochondrial genes. This finding is hypothesis-generating and warrants experimental validation, but could represent a novel combination strategy: SMARCA2 degradation/inhibition combined with agents targeting mitochondrial function.

### EZH2 as a combination target in ovarian cancer

The strong EZH2 SL signal in Ovary/Fallopian Tube (d = −1.96, FDR = 0.011) is biologically plausible. EZH2 (Polycomb Repressive Complex 2) has a well-established antagonistic relationship with SWI/SNF complexes in chromatin regulation. When SWI/SNF is compromised by SMARCA4 loss, cells may become more dependent on PRC2/EZH2 for chromatin regulation. Approved EZH2 inhibitors (tazemetostat) could potentially be repurposed for SMARCA4-deficient ovarian cancers, particularly SCCOHT. The CRISPR-PRISM concordance for CPI-1205 in Ovary/FT (r = −0.354, p = 0.029) provides modest pharmacological support.

### Clinical translation: a narrowing but focused path

The atlas strongly validates the current clinical strategy of targeting SMARCA2 in SMARCA4-mutant NSCLC. Lung cancer has both the strongest SL signal and the largest patient population (~6,247 LOF patients/year in the US). However, the therapeutic landscape has contracted: with PRT7732 paused and FHD-609 targeting a different biology (SMARCB1/synovial sarcoma), FHD-909 (Foghorn/Lilly) is the sole actively advancing SMARCA2-targeted program for SMARCA4-mutant cancers. The >90% SMARCA2 target degradation achieved by PRT7732 before its pause suggests the biology is druggable; the setback appears to be financial/strategic rather than scientific.

### Comparison with ARID1A Atlas

Both SMARCA4 and ARID1A are SWI/SNF complex subunits, but their SL profiles differ:

- **SMARCA4:** Strong SMARCA2 paralog SL in Lung; EZH2 SL in Ovary; mitochondrial vulnerability
- **ARID1A:** Broader tissue-type distribution of SL effects; distinct SL gene networks

This divergence is expected — SMARCA4/SMARCA2 are catalytic ATPase subunits (paralog relationship), while ARID1A is a DNA-binding specificity subunit. Their loss creates different functional consequences and dependencies.

## Limitations

1. **Sample size constraints.** The 88 SMARCA4-deficient DepMap lines are distributed unevenly across cancer types. Only Lung (36 deficient, 25 with CRISPR data) approaches adequate statistical power. Results in Ovary (8–9 lines), Lymphoid (3–8), and Bowel (1–5) must be interpreted cautiously.

2. **Class 2 analysis not feasible.** With only 15 Class 2 (missense) lines total and <3 per cancer type, it was not possible to determine whether missense mutations confer SL sensitivity. PRT3789 clinical data suggest they do not (responses only in Class 1), but this cannot be confirmed computationally.

3. **No independent validation.** The project auto-advanced through the analysis and validation pipeline stages without dedicated analyst or validation scientist review. Results should be considered preliminary until externally validated.

4. **PRISM drug coverage gap.** Only 2 of 13 target drugs were available, both EZH2 inhibitors. SMARCA2 degraders (the drugs most directly relevant to the SL hypothesis) are not in PRISM. BET, HDAC, ATR, and CDK4/6 inhibitors were also absent, preventing pharmacological validation of several SL candidates.

5. **Cell line vs. patient biology.** DepMap cell lines are cultured in vitro and may not fully recapitulate the tumor microenvironment, immune interactions, and stromal context that influence SL relationships in patients.

6. **TCGA SCCOHT underrepresentation.** The zero LOF patient estimate for Ovary/FT likely reflects that SCCOHT — the tumor type most associated with SMARCA4 loss — is rare and poorly represented in TCGA. The true SMARCA4-deficient ovarian cancer population may be larger than estimated.

7. **No co-mutation analysis performed.** The plan included analysis of co-occurring mutations (KRAS, TP53, STK11, KEAP1) and their effect on SL relationships. This was not implemented in the current pipeline.

## Next Steps

1. **Experimental validation of mitochondrial SL in ovarian SMARCA4-deficient models.** The mitochondrial vulnerability finding (7/9 top Ovary/FT SL genes) is novel and could be validated using mitochondrial-targeting agents (e.g., IACS-010759, gamitrinib) in SMARCA4-deficient SCCOHT cell lines.

2. **EZH2 inhibitor combination studies.** Test tazemetostat (approved EZH2 inhibitor) in SMARCA4-deficient ovarian cancer models. If confirmed, this could provide a rapid path to clinical testing given tazemetostat's existing approval for epithelioid sarcoma and follicular lymphoma.

3. **Track FHD-909 Phase 1 data.** As the only actively advancing SMARCA2-targeted therapy, FHD-909 efficacy data by tumor type will be the key external validation point for the atlas SL rankings.

4. **Expand Class 2 analysis.** As DepMap adds more cell lines, revisit whether SMARCA4 Class 2 (missense) mutations confer meaningful SL dependencies. This has direct clinical relevance for patient selection.

5. **Co-mutation stratification.** Implement the planned KRAS, TP53, STK11, and KEAP1 co-mutation analysis, particularly in lung cancer where these co-mutations are common and clinically relevant.

6. **Cross-atlas integration.** Formally compare SMARCA4 and ARID1A SL networks to identify shared SWI/SNF vulnerabilities vs. subunit-specific dependencies.
